Commentary on the modern state of restaurants. 
<!--more-->

## thoughts

> Salt, salt, salt...

Restaurants are stealing a page from McDonald's playbook and purposefully adding too much salt to force you to order drinks that are overpriced.

Why?

It's because they have more profit from drinks for psychological reasons.

If a steak costs \$50, then a drink for \$10, \$15, or even \$20 doesn't look terrible especially when your thirst is quenched by the overabundant sodium thrown your way.

This is primarily American, but I have seen this in Seoul and more dense cities in Asia. I hate this. Restaurants used to be focused on delivering better products but with rents, logistics, and perhaps external pressure from investors, they are forced to play the grimy game of riping customers off rather than providing good service.

> How can this be resolved?

I don't know. The issue in the states is that bullshit jobs are abundant and pay multiples more than service jobs and zoning laws make mom-and-pop shops nil compared with Tokyo for instance.

Because we invest in indices that create concentrated conglomerates, we are forced to go with cheap global solutions instead of building up camaraderie with our local communities and suppy chains.

The resolution is to be more cognizant of where your money goes. "Easy" solutions like throwing retirement money in the S&P 500 or Vanguard ETFs is equivalent to throwing up the middle finger to peers around you. You are saying "I like these global, cheap solutions" but then these companies squeeze out any diversity/competition and rejack prices and the cycle of capitalism continues. I think we're better than this or at least can be but it starts with individual reflection and cognition. Be deliberate and aware of where your money, time, and attention flows to. You can build up your community surprisingly quickly if you shut out particular channels and learn to say no to convenience.
