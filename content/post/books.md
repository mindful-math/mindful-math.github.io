## nonfiction


| Title | Score | Thoughts |
|------|------|----------|
| [Algorithms for Convex Optimization](https://convex-optimization.github.io/) | 6/10 | I think the book is great for a semester-long second course in optimization. I think you need calculus, some familiarity with proofs, linear algebra, and preferably a course in linear programming or a first course on the subject to follow. I liked the exercises but remember being confused and resorting to Nesterov's book for acccelerated gradient descent - very confusing analysis there. |
| [Lectures on Convex Optimization](https://link.springer.com/book/10.1007/978-3-319-91578-4) | 6/10 | I cannot pretend to have read it end-to-end in detail but it is a great reference. In particular, the first chapter was nice, his bits on Newton's method, interior-point methods, and subgradient methods were fabulous but my god dude, make this a magnum opus and add some rich exercises. Nesterov is one of the GOATs here and I wish he'd cut research short to make this more approachable to noobs. |
| [Probability and Measure](https://www.wiley.com/en-us/Probability+and+Measure%2C+Anniversary+Edition-p-9781118122372) | 7/10 | I took this course way too early - like my sophmore year of college and could not appreciate this book in totality. Billingsley does a phenomenal job here with exercises and maintaining a story. I found later sections on stochastic processes fairly unapproachable but our professor ([Lyons](https://rdlyons.pages.iu.edu/)) did a great job filling in the gaps. |
| [An Introduction to Probability Theory and Its Applications Volume I](https://www.amazon.com/Introduction-Probability-Theory-Applications-Vol/dp/0471257087) | 10/10 | This is my favorite book recommended by my first probability professor as a more difficult supplemental read. Feller is so fucking good and I just had a blast reading his exposition (flowed unbelievably well) and was followed by rich exercises... It is majorly underappreciated and no book comes close to this - I became pretty darn adept with combinatorics thanks to doing all the exercises here. |
| [Linear Statistical Inference and Its Applications](https://www.amazon.com/Linear-Statistical-Inference-its-Applications/dp/0471218758) | 7/10 | Another very good exposition - I think Rao did a phenomenal job making the book self-contained by introducing relevant calculus / linear algebra and I liked the sections on probability distributions. To be totally honest, I never made it through the actual statistics sections completely. Perhaps I am biased, but I just find statistics largely useless despite getting a Masters degree in the subject. Basically, read this for the probablity / math and skim through statistics portions that are of interest to you. |
| [All of Statistics](https://www.amazon.com/All-Statistics-Statistical-Inference-Springer/dp/0387402721) | 5/10 | The sections on parametric inference and hypothesis testing are not bad but like it's such a boring read and the exercises are not at all tied to applications (which make statistics worthwhile...) and I found the exposition read like shortform wikipedia pages that are disjointly connected / numerated. |
| [Statistical Models: Theory and Practice](https://www.amazon.com/Statistical-Models-Practice-David-Freedman/dp/0521743850) | 7/10 | If you are interested in econometrics, sociology, etc., then this is a great read. The author has very good intuition and has obviously taught many courses in statistics and has edited this book many times over. This is the right balance of theory and practice - he talks about i.i.d. assumptions and alternatives and teaches the important pieces you should know. With that said, I think people do not respect or care for this theory anymore aside from academics Most experts rely on trial and tribulation and expertise gained through experiencing things. |
| [Calcul Différentiel](https://openlibrary.org/works/OL42268724W/Calcul_diff%C3%A9rentiel) | 6/10 | It's a great read but the book cover makes it difficult to hold without messing up the spine of the book - organized like Rudin's series on Analysis. |
| [Art of Computer Programming: Sorting and Searching](https://www.amazon.com/Art-Computer-Programming-Sorting-Searching/dp/0201896850) | 5.5/10 | I really am indifferent to this book. I love rated exercises and solutions - a fantastic idea. I loathe his exposition - I found it incredibly boring and did not find his exposition clear. I actually do not like how much he cites other people - it really detracts from the flow of the book. I didn't finish the book FYI but feel as though I did enough exercises and read enough to write a review. |
| [Principles of Operations Research](https://www.amazon.com/Principles-Operations-Research-Applications-Managerial/dp/0137095929) | 6.5/10 | While the techniques are outdated, I was shocked by how easy to read this was and how interesting it was that the author grounded the theory in actual applications and examples. Sales forecasting, sensitivity testing, etc. are so interesting and rich with examples. |
| [Advanced Calculus](https://www.amazon.com/Advanced-Calculus-Revised-Harold-Loomis/dp/9814583936) | 7/10 | Wow. This book is complete, rigorous and well-organized in flow. I found it difficult compared with more standard analysis books (Rudin, Bartle, etc.), but much more comprehensive with interesting extensions in later sections on manifolds, exterior calculus, and differential equations. It would work nicely for a year-long course but way too dense for a semester. |
| [Network Flows](https://www.amazon.com/Network-Flows-Theory-Algorithms-Applications/dp/013617549X) | ?/10 | This is the dual version of "Principles of Operations Research". I have not read it and find it abstract and difficult to approach for self-study while working and studying other areas but it was recommended by one of my friends who is a rockstar in the space. Still unsure if it will just accumulate dust on my bookshelf... |
| [Time Series Analysis](https://www.amazon.com/Time-Analysis-James-Douglas-Hamilton/dp/0691042896) | 6/10 | This is a chunky book - dude poured his heart and soul into crafting it. Applications are non-existent and this is more of an encyclopedia than a book for a course on the matter despite the author being an economist. I found its sections on ARIMA and related tools quite interesting and challenging but not very insightful. My main gripe is just how equation heavy it is. Unlike Feller and masterful works, this text boils down to reading an equation, deriving the next equation, and seeing a bunch of disjointed references to find a solution - it feels clunky (which time series is to be honest). I will give it a few more reads and then probably trash/donate. |
| [Basic Econometrics](https://www.amazon.com/Basic-Econometrics-Damodar-Gujarati/dp/0072478527) | ?/10 | This one is accruing dust on my shelf. It is like Hamilton's book but much shorter with more vivid exposition (at least on the surface). I just cannot justify learning this theory as it's not very useful to be honest... |
| [Scheduling: Theory, Algorithms, and Systems](https://www.amazon.com/Scheduling-Algorithms-Michael-L-Pinedo/dp/1461419867) | 5/10 | I love the topic but this is purely academic hogwash with little applications for a subject entirely dependent on applications. What is the point in assuming order flows are Poisson, Exponential, etc. and what happens if they don't? It's useless and I haven't been able to read it end-to-end. |
| [Probabilistic Method](https://www.amazon.com/Probabilistic-Method-Noga-Alon/dp/0470170204) | 6.5/10 | Noga Alon is a GOAT in the space and the perfect person to describe the probabilistic method. I love the examples and exercises but I find exercises lacking and think some of the proofs are a bit terse and not from the book as Paul would say :) |
| [Naive Set Theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naive_Set_Theory_(book)) | 7/10 | Halmos is a master expositor - those mathematicians born in the early 1900s must have eaten a lot of spinach or something magical because everything is so clear and approachable. I miss how simple and elegant these texts are - the folk are not on TikTok and have much better attention spans. |
| [Theoretical Statistics](https://www.amazon.com/Theoretical-Statistics-Topics-Course-Springer/dp/0387938389) | 5/10 | I mean this is like the equivalent of Rudin's series on analysis for statistics but personally I struggled with motivation. It's so dry, so incredibly dry and I didn't end up finishing all the chapters. It is theoretical statistics but like Rao said in his book (and I wholeheartedly agree with), statistics is meaningless unless married with another subject and I worry this text doesn't instill that mindset... |
| [Principles of Combinatorics](https://www.amazon.com/Principles-Combinatorics-C-Berge/dp/0124109780) | 6/10 | I actually liked this book a lot. He derives counting tactics methodically and it's quite playful. The big gap in the book is the lack of exercises... like why dude - it's expository material and you could make this into a nice book for students trying to learn combinatorics. |


* I did not end up reading Feller's second volume as I got lost quite early on and unmotivated - I could not find a course that structured itself off the book.
