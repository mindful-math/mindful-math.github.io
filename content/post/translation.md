<!--more-->

## definitions

> Formal methods are mathematically rigorous techniques for the specification, development, analysis, and verification of software and hardware systems [[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formal_methods).


> [Formal logic] uses a formal approach to study reasoning: it replaces concrete expressions with abstract symbols to examine the logical form of arguments independent of their concrete content. In this sense, it is topic-neutral since it is only concerned with the abstract structure of arguments and not with their concrete content. Formal logic is interested in deductively valid arguments, for which the truth of their premises ensures the truth of their conclusion. [[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logic#Formal_logic)

> Language is a structured system of communication that consists of grammar and vocabulary. It is the primary means by which humans convey meaning, both in spoken and signed forms, and may also be conveyed through writing [[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Language).

## thoughts

- LLMs and recent models have been developing wonderful capabilities at translating language X to Y. This includes programming languages, languages we speak, and many others. I think this capacity is the most powerful tool to emerge from LLMs as a whole. This allows a lay person to ask thoughtful questions they once thought impossible or improbable given then heavy burden or pile of books needed to conquer first. This ability is also what scares many engineers as that is exactly the task they often work off of: business person hands them abstract idea 1 and then they are forced to twist and tune that idea 1 iteratively into something a computer can work with logically.
- I really think getting a decent translation of the task done with an LLM goes a long way towards a solution. It allows people to test out their half-baked ideas and if they particularly enjoy them, they can work with professionals to round out the remaining 20% of the project or so and actually make a polished work. It's like the equivalent of an auto home-building tool (I'm serious); it allows people to ask in their language (English, Chinese, etc.) and get out an approximate home that maintains the functionality they desire. SO OFTEN, we get caught up in small details like coverage or quirks in syntax for different languages and forget that the people paying us are just interested in ideas that help make their lives easier. I am so for this technology from this point of view.
- The main issue will be that later versions of models will be caked in ads and other crap while we have local ollama and related tools available to use that aren't perfect but again suite the need of most non-technical folk...
- This has honestly been this way for quite some time. People don't need our brain children or fantastical ideas pushing the boundary of science; they just need something useful for themselves (asking Gemini about some weird bruising on their legs or a website for their business or ...). Power will come from the connections you have with others (meaningful connections) but I'm pretty confused on money/growth for young folk in this scenario (it feels like old people spend less, live longer, and young have to take care???). That part is fuzzy at best and my biggest fear personally (I just see less and less growth possible) and the government is increasingly bent on supporting capitalism despite opportunity drying up. Anyways back to formal methods...
- Humans use imprecise language, LLMs make that slightly more precise (not perfect, but CLOSE enough) and them SMT solvers, etc. can do some magic to provide guarantees to users (to prevent litigation concerns / etc.).

> Is there then a need to understand logic and formal methods given LLMs allow us to translate quite well?

I think this same question is asked for every field touched by LLMs: engineers, mathematicians, etc. My gut tells me yes but only a handful. These technologies really ask for a select few academic folk to man the system and make sure it behaves properly and update it as need be and then the rest (the translators between formal and informal, experimenters, etc.) are all left to find different means of providing value to society. What is funny is that we still need the person to ask the right questions to the system but this is a much lower barrier to entry than beforehand. In the future, your translators, engineers, etc. are either forced to be 10x and work in pretty grueling conditions with the technologies they love or participate in more soul-sucking corporate jobs w/ others for dinero.
